Binding is the process of connecting or securing objects together to form a single unit. In physical contexts, this can involve tying, stitching, or gluing materials, such as in bookbinding or packaging. In computing, binding refers to linking functions, variables, or resources to specific contexts or values, ensuring that programs execute correctly and data is properly associated with operations. The concept is central to both structural integrity and functional reliability.

In bookbinding, the method chosen affects durability and usability. Traditional techniques use thread and glue to attach pages to a spine, producing long-lasting books. Modern methods may employ thermal or adhesive binding, allowing faster production but often sacrificing longevity. Beyond books, binding principles apply to other materials, such as textiles, wires, or polymers, where maintaining cohesion and alignment is essential for performance and safety.

In software development, binding can be static or dynamic. Static binding associates functions or variables at compile time, improving performance but reducing flexibility. Dynamic binding resolves associations at runtime, supporting polymorphism and adaptable behavior. Understanding binding mechanisms is critical for programmers to manage memory, execution order, and interaction between components, ensuring systems operate efficiently and predictably.
